<p>I attended <a href="https://www.are.na/are-na-editorial/channel-walkthrough-with-daniel-lefcourt-and-travess-smalley" rel="noopener">a virtual Are.na walkthrough</a>: <a href="https://www.travesssmalley.com/" rel="noopener">Travess Smalley</a> &amp; <a href="https://certainlynot.com/" rel="noopener">Daniel Lefcourt</a>. They discussed how they use it to develop their ideas.</p>
<ul>
    <li>Unrelated fields overlap in unexpected ways: new insights in disparate connections.</li>
    <li>Save everything you find interesting. You never know how it might inspire you.</li>
    <li>If there ins't a word to describe what you're exploring, there's opportunity there.</li>
    <li>Art is a language. And just as we study books to find how we write, we can study artworks to discover how we express ourselves.</li>
    <li>Each painting sings the same song, uniquely.</li>
    <li>When you discover something new, you want to find others like them. If the creators share their influences, you'll discover something unique. So share yours.</li>
    <li>The beauty of mixed media is you can mix and match often separate senses for surprising results. For example, sheet music as artworks.</li>
    <li>Learn the rules of software to break them to make art with unusual constraints.</li>
    <li>Rules are freeing when overwhelmed by the limitless of creativity, but breaking them and creating beyond them is often where the most fascinating ideas come from.</li>
    <li>If you're stuck on a problem, working it out on paper can lead to breakthroughs.</li>
    <li>Our taste influences what we make. Begin with what you love and build upon it.</li>
    <li>The computer is a creative assistant, a portal to inspiration, or an art medium. Experiment.</li>
    <li>Creative processes as programming scripts.</li>
    <li>Book or zine making is such a rewarding process as it allows for constant experimentation.</li>
    <li>Paper is an excellent tool for processing information and engaging with a work.</li>
</ul>
