Creativity In The Unexpected

Published: 24 Feb 2025 | Updated: 24 Feb 2025

Going through my notes in the last while, I found jottings worth sharing from events attended. I thought I'd organize them and share them here.

Amanda Montell's Author Talk (23 Jan 2025)

Attending this (online) was a delight. Her answers to the moderator and audience's questions were insightful, caring and enthusiastic. An incisive thinker!

  • She got her start in socio-linguistics (studying language in culture/society). She wanted to become a dialect coach or a pop linguist.
  • Combining your interests creates the most interesting work.
  • Switching between writing non-fiction and fiction provides constant inspiration and interest.
  • Humor makes heavier topics easier to approach, but balance delight and sensitivity.
  • Sometimes writers use humor as a defense to their detriment.
  • Asking a college professor for their reading assignments in a subject you want to study gives you a great starting point for research.
  • Make the research process interesting to get yourself excited about a writing project.
  • Interviews with experts + unexpected fields of knowledge + cultural relevancy + social commentary creates fascinating creative non-fiction.
  • Information overload and cognitive biases are on a dramatic collision course; critical thinking and letting go of needing to be right combats the onslaught.
  • Skepticism needs to apply to your thoughts.
  • While optimism won't fix your life, but it'll make it much easier to live.

Creativity In Drought Research Panel (24 Aug 2024)

Went to this in person. A fascinating discussion that turned into a conversation with the audience, hence why I didn't take many notes!

  • The visual tropes of irrigated settlements create a romantic notion of drought immunity.
  • People assume they have control over nature: they can make the desert bloom.
  • Apiarists sometimes hire out their bees to pollinate fruit and nut trees.
  • Interdisciplinary research creates the richest insights and gives you a deeper understanding: combine the humanities and the sciences for the best results.

EWF x Sinister Wisdom Panel (9 Sep 2024)

This panel, hosted as part of the Emerging Writers Festival 2024 was a conversation with folks involved in Sinister Wisdom, the longest running lesbian literary journal.

Featuring: Pelaya Arapakis, Parla Tejada, Yasmin Tambiah, Joan Nestle, and Julie Enszer.

  • Transnationalism bridges borders: real and invented. Hope for the future.
  • The more cultures involved in a collaborative creative project, the richer the result.
  • Publishing texts for an international audience requires humility and curiosity.
  • Doing it for the love allows greater experimentation.
  • Archiving as an art form.
  • When creating a print publication, using index cards to represent the working structure is a great way to design.
  • Cross-cultural exchange is a rich opportunity for inspiring new writing.
  • Argumentative discussions as productive disagreements.
  • Generational divides are boundaries made to be broken.
  • When making connections with elders, don't assume there'll be difficulties, but recognize there'll be learning moments.
  • To learn is to listen without judgement.
  • Comparable experiences allow for addressing most differences.
  • Fear of the unknown kills the chance for creative collaboration.
  • Published works break time, they freeze it, and exist outside it.
  • Every time someone picks up and reads a published work, they're experiencing it for the first time. Keep them in mind alongside your longtime readers and those you write for.
  • Writers need a place to write, a home to send their words to.
  • Change for a brighter future is never finished. The work must always go on.
  • Independent publishing fills in the gaps left by mainstream efforts. The more voices in print we have, the richer the culture.
  • There's no such thing as too many words published.
  • Writing as a conduit for new possibilities.
  • Older folks being visible in our society means everything to younger people with shared experiences.
  • In intergenerational friendships, you can look to the future and the past.
  • Life is a paradox: hope as tragedy, tragedy as hope. Everything changes, yet stays the same.
  • We stay alive through celebrating difference.
  • Stay open to everyday magic.
  • Be open-minded, but not so much so your brain falls out. It's a balance.

Liane Moriarty's Author Talk (22 Sep 2024)

This writer, one of the world's bestsellers, is from Australia! I haven't read her books but found her craft and publishing perspectives fascinating.

  • Early encouragement means everything in creativity. Unfounded criticism kills it.
  • Often the most suspenseful stories come from taking one ordinary thing and looking at it differently or adding surrealism.
  • Writing word by word without a plan is thrilling but also enticing in its uncertainty.
  • Tracking words written and combining it with intermittent rewards equals excellent motivation.
  • Write the draft in full and keep a list of things to fix in to avoid distraction.
  • Themes in written works aren't planned, they emerge.
  • Recording your search history can be a fascinating exercise in personal archiving.
  • When creating a character, start with an interesting attribute or two.
  • Characters evolve over the writing. You can add consistency later.
  • Words have incredible power. Use them wisely.
  • Never use the same character name twice in the same book, ensure sure no two characters' names start with the same initial.
  • Give people the chance to bid to have a name of their choosing in a book-in-progress in a charity auction.

XOXO 2024 Talks

Many thanks to the generous founders of this conference for publishing the recordings online. How I appreciated hearing from the speakers.

Caleb Sasser

  • It's not just about the work. We want to be seen. We want to live on after we die.
  • Appreciate everything. Stop and take the time to experience something.
  • Tell everyone how much you appreciate them and their work. Send the nice email now before it's too late.
  • Put up a portfolio. Don't throw yourself or your work away. Let people see what you did.
  • Go down all the research rabbit holes. You never know what you might discover.
  • Save the art you love. For yourself and the future.

Charlie Jane Anders

  • Daydreaming is the opposite of doomscrolling. It's crucial work.
  • Learned helplessness is the fastest way to destroy hope.
  • There's never nothing you can do. There's always something positive you can contribute.
  • Community is hope for the future.
  • Creating community creates a consensus reality: a future we can look forward to.
  • Create the reality you want to see in the world and you never have to do it alone.
  • Futurism needs community, vice versa.
  • There's this idea events are temporary, and the written word is forever when the opposite can be true. And that's not a bad thing.
  • Recognizing your art may not last forever takes the pressure off. Experiment. Make it strange.
  • What's progress if not finding new stories?
  • When you're freed from speaking only to posterity, you're free to speak to the moment: Creating the future for yourself and others here now.
  • Wanting certainty is fragile. The only way to survive is to fall in love with being wrong.
  • The hardest things to talk about are the most important. Be brave, and step up.
  • Creativity is communal. Keep expanding the circle.
  • Getting uncomfortable is how we grow.
  • Unrelenting grimness is unrealistic.
  • Nothing makes sense. And that's okay. We need silliness.
  • Kindness saves us. It's why we're here.
  • Embrace gentle absurdity.

Ed Yong

  • Journalism often covers the smaller parts of a bigger story: piecing it all together is difficult but necessary work. It's called evidence-based journalism of the imagination.
  • Writing good non-fiction often means finding new and novel sources than anything else in the canon table, because this allows for richer reporting.
  • Nonfiction about societal problems is a collective narrative, showing how a central protagonist POV destroys the complexity.
  • Sense-making is synthesizing.
  • We can always make better choices by using our moral information.
  • Creativity is always the work of many.
  • Feeding the system of attention-grabbing is the antithesis of nuanced rigor.
  • Rules for a sane existence online: no arguing with strangers, don't become a pundit, cultivate readers, not fans, no savior complex, acknowledge and uplift community, act the part even if you don't feel it, use power well.
  • Use your insights to tell the stories that aren't being told.
  • Journalism channels empathy, and brings words to the indescribable.
  • The people who read a piece aren't the only ones who're affected by them. Words start conversations, opening the door to understanding and change.
